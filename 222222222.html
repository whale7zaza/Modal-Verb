Modal Verbs

Guide to Understanding Modal Verbs

What are Modal Verbs?

Modal verbs are auxiliary verbs that express necessity, possibility, permission, ability, or advice. They are used to modify the meaning of the main verb in a sentence.

Examples of modal verbs include: can, could, may, might, shall, should, will, would, must.

Examples:

She can swim very well.

We must finish this project by tomorrow.

He might come to the party later.

Characteristics of Modal Verbs

Modal verbs have unique characteristics that set them apart from other verbs:

  • They do not change form (no -s, -ed, or -ing endings).
  • They are followed by the base form of the main verb (infinitive without "to").
  • They do not require auxiliary verbs like "do/does" in questions or negatives.

Modal verbs are essential for expressing different levels of certainty, obligation, and possibility.

Examples:

✅ She can play the piano.

❌ She cans play the piano.

Modal Verb: Can

The modal verb can is used to express:

  • Ability: I can swim.
  • Permission: Can I use your phone?
  • Possibility: It can be dangerous.

Using "can" helps you communicate skills, opportunities, or permissions in daily life.

Examples:

He can drive a car.

Can you help me?

Modal Verb: Must

The modal verb must is used to express:

  • Obligation: You must wear a seatbelt.
  • Necessity: We must complete the report by Friday.
  • Strong probability: It must be raining outside; I hear thunder.

Examples:

You must finish your homework before going out.

She must be the new teacher; everyone is greeting her.
